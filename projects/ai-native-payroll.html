An AI-native product for payroll

Designing what payroll software looks like when AI sits inside the workflow, not bolted on top.

Role Product Designer and Builder
Industry AI-Native B2B SaaS · HR Payroll
Year 2026 — Now

This is a real project, shown in an anonymized form. Product name, brand, proprietary flows and most data are under NDA — the screens below are a redesigned, demo version with placeholder data, made to show the thinking without exposing anything confidential.

The bet

In Italy, payroll software is built for one demanding user: the consulente del lavoro. A single consultant runs payroll for dozens of client companies, tracks regulation that changes monthly, and works across legacy screens that reward expertise and punish almost everything else.

The bet is narrow and testable: AI shouldn’t sit in a side panel — it should change the shape of the task. The real design challenge isn’t the model, it’s the user’s role: moving consultants and payroll specialists from operators to supervisors of work the AI does. That only works if the human stays in the loop and trusts the output — so every screen has to earn that trust.

I joined an experimental team in 2026 to help build one of the first AI-native payroll products in Italy, working alongside product, engineering, and domain experts.

A few things we designed

Three flows, each taken from problem to working prototype — and each built around the same principle: AI proposes, the expert decides.

1. An entry point organized by intent, not by modules

The home screen replaces a dashboard of modules with one composer (“type or dictate — use @ to pick a company or an employee”) and two layers of scaffolding underneath. Quick actions name what the product can do; proactive suggestions surface the day’s real work, pulled from the consultant’s own book of business — “5 companies with record changes to handle”, “2 companies ready for automated payslip verification” — each card tagged with the client it touches.

The decision underneath: an expert tool can’t open on a blank chat box. The suggestions do double duty — they teach the surface and front-load the work, so the first screen is already useful, not just inviting.

2. Document extraction with side-by-side review

Onboarding a new client means building its company record from an official registry document — the most data-dense step in the flow. I designed it as a two-pane review: source document on the left, structured record on the right. The AI states what it did in plain language, gives a short summary, and ends on an explicit decision — do you want to proceed?

This is the supervisor pattern in miniature: every field stays editable before saving, the source stays visible for cross-checking, and the terminal actions are unambiguous. No silent autofill; no trust without verification.

3. A generated deliverable the consultant can actually send

A plain request (“show me this client’s cost trend, Q1”) becomes a client-ready report on the consultant’s own letterhead: total cost, breakdown by component, and a chart by month — rendered in the company’s custom template, with one fixed action: send to client as attachment.

Two decisions defined this screen. The chat-side summary is a decision aid, not a wall of text — the headline number, the breakdown, and an offer to go deeper. And the output is treated as a real artifact, not a chat bubble: a fixed header and a fixed action bar frame the scrollable document, so a deliverable always looks like a deliverable.

How we work

Small team, builder mindset, AI-driven process end to end. We use AI tools across the whole lifecycle — from ideation and brainstorming through development — and we ship to learn: build, test, learn, without waiting for perfection to show the work. To validate IA and conversation logic in days, I built clickable prototypes with Claude Code and Figma Make, so the team could react to a real flow instead of a static mockup.

I sit at the intersection of product design and product management — mapping IA, writing specs and acceptance criteria, prioritizing MVP scope with the PM, and keeping the line tight between a design decision and what ships.

Why it matters

This is one of the few genuine attempts to design payroll software AI-first, not AI-bolted-on — and the work is as much about people as pixels: helping experts evolve into supervisors of AI without losing authorship of the result.

It’s the same instinct that led me to start a long-term-care venture inside the Politecnico di Torino incubator (i3P): I build things meant to have real impact on people — products, but also services and initiatives.
