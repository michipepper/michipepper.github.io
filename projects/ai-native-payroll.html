An AI-native product for payroll

Designing payroll software where AI lives inside the workflow — not bolted on top.

Role Product Designer and Builder
Industry AI-Native B2B SaaS · HR Payroll
Year 2026 — Now

A real project, shown anonymized. Product name, brand, and proprietary flows are under NDA. The screens below are a redesigned demo with placeholder data — enough to show the thinking, nothing confidential.

The bet

In Italy, payroll software serves one demanding user: the consulente del lavoro. One consultant runs payroll for dozens of client companies, tracks regulation that shifts monthly, and works across legacy screens that reward expertise and punish everything else.

The bet is narrow and testable: AI shouldn’t sit in a side panel — it should change the shape of the task. The hard part isn’t the model, it’s the user’s role: moving consultants and payroll specialists from operators to supervisors of work the AI does. That only holds if the human stays in the loop and trusts the output — so every screen has to earn that trust.

I joined an experimental team in 2026 to help build one of Italy’s first AI-native payroll products, alongside product, engineering, and domain experts.

A few things we designed

Three flows, each taken from problem to working prototype, all built on one principle: AI proposes, the expert decides.

1. An entry point organized by intent, not by modules

The home screen drops the module dashboard for one composer (“type or dictate — use @ to pick a company or an employee”) over two layers of scaffolding. Quick actions name what the product can do; proactive suggestions surface the day’s real work, pulled from the consultant’s own book of business — “5 companies with record changes to handle”, “2 companies ready for automated payslip verification” — each card tagged with the client it touches.

The call underneath: an expert tool can’t open on a blank chat box. The suggestions do double duty — they teach the surface and front-load the work, so the first screen is useful, not just inviting.

2. Document extraction with side-by-side review

Onboarding a client means building its company record from an official registry document — the most data-dense step in the flow. I designed it as a two-pane review: source document left, structured record right. The AI states what it did in plain language, gives a short summary, and ends on one explicit decision — proceed?

This is the supervisor pattern in miniature: every field editable before saving, the source visible for cross-checking, terminal actions unambiguous. No silent autofill. No trust without verification.

3. A generated deliverable the consultant can actually send

A plain request (“show me this client’s cost trend, Q1”) becomes a client-ready report on the consultant’s own letterhead: total cost, breakdown by component, a chart by month — rendered in the company’s template, with one fixed action: send to client as attachment.

Two decisions shaped this screen. The chat-side summary is a decision aid, not a wall of text — headline number, breakdown, an offer to go deeper. And the output is treated as a real artifact, not a chat bubble: fixed header, fixed action bar, scrollable document. A deliverable should look like a deliverable.

How we work

Small team, builder mindset, AI-driven end to end. We use AI tools across the whole lifecycle — ideation through development — and ship to learn: build, test, learn, without waiting for perfection. To validate IA and conversation logic in days, I built clickable prototypes in Claude Code and Figma Make, so the team could react to a real flow, not a static mockup.

I sit between product design and product management — mapping IA, writing specs and acceptance criteria, prioritizing MVP scope with the PM, keeping the line tight between a design decision and what ships.

Why it matters

This is one of the few real attempts to design payroll software AI-first, not AI-bolted-on — and the work is as much about people as pixels: helping experts become supervisors of AI without losing authorship of the result.

Same instinct that led me to start a long-term-care venture inside the Politecnico di Torino incubator (i3P): I build things meant to have real impact on people — products, but also services and initiatives.
