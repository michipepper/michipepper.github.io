An AI-native product for payroll

Designing what payroll software looks like when AI sits inside the workflow, not bolted on top.

Role Product Designer (team of 3)
Industry AI-Native B2B SaaS · HR / Payroll
Year 2025 — Now
Status Active · pre-launch

The bet

Payroll software in Italy is built around a very specific user: the consulente del lavoro — the labor consultant who manages payroll for dozens of client companies, navigates ever-changing regulations, and lives inside spreadsheets and legacy interfaces. The tools they use today are powerful but unforgiving. They reward expertise and punish almost everything else.

The bet behind this product is that AI changes the shape of the workflow itself — not just the UI on top of it. If a labor consultant could converse with the software, ask it to draft, check, and explain things, the whole product surface needs to be rethought from the ground up.

I joined this initiative inside TeamSystem in 2025, as part of a three-person design team. We’re building this in close collaboration with product, engineering, and domain experts.

Where I’ve focused

The product spans many surfaces — here’s what I’ve owned or co-owned so far.

Information architecture for the new anagrafica

The anagrafica (employee, company, and branch registries) is the foundation of any payroll product — and the most data-dense part. I mapped the full IA across three core screens (Scheda azienda, Scheda filiale, Scheda dipendente), defining tab structures, field clusters, and a custom “UI reuse” status to track which components carried over from the legacy system and which needed to be redesigned for the AI-native experience.

This work was the bridge between the legacy AZE platform and the new product — tagging fields with MEP/MVP priorities across ten tabs, and aligning the team on what shipped first.

Rapid prototyping with AI-assisted tools

To validate IA hypotheses and navigation logic in days instead of weeks, I built interactive prototypes using Claude Code and Figma Make. One prototype covered the multi-level navigation (company → branch → employee), a four-step employee-creation modal, breadcrumb logic, and the topbar/sidebar shell pulled from the Figma design kit.

The prototypes weren’t throwaway. They became a shared artifact the team could click through, react to, and challenge — which moved decisions forward faster than static mockups could have.

AI conversation design

The most strategic part of the work: figuring out what AI actually does inside the product. Not chatbot answers — but how a conversation with the software helps a labor consultant generate, for example, a UniEmens declaration, or check that a contract aligns with the latest regulation (think ViDA and similar regulatory shifts).

I researched AI applications in payroll workflows specifically, and explored conversation patterns suited to expert users who don’t want a friendly assistant — they want a precise, fast, accountable collaborator.

UX decision-making frameworks

I also worked on a few foundational UX questions that come up over and over in a product like this: when to use a full-page modal vs. a drawer, how to apply progressive disclosure to dense data, and how to balance power-user shortcuts with onboarding clarity. These small decisions add up — in a tool experts will live inside for hours a day, they define whether the product feels respectful of their time or not.

How we work

This is a small team operating with a builder mindset. We move fast, we prototype early, we let the work speak. I sit at the intersection of product design and product management — mapping IA, writing specs, defining acceptance criteria, prioritizing alongside the PM, and keeping the connection between design decisions and what gets shipped.

Process is light. The output is what matters.

Why this matters

This product is one of the most strategic initiatives in its category inside the company — and one of the few real attempts to design payroll software AI-first rather than AI-bolted-on. The opportunity isn’t the AI itself; it’s rethinking the whole product surface around it.

Working on it has sharpened how I think about AI-native architecture, conversation design, and what it means to design software for experts in an AI-first world.
